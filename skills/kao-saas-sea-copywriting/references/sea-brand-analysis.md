## Grab

**Category:** Super-app (ride-hailing, delivery, payments, financial services)
**Markets:** Pan-SEA
**Audience:** Consumers and drivers across all demographics
**Why their copy works:**

**Grade 6 readability with warm, community-centered copy:**
> "Making every day better"

Simple, aspirational, and universal. No jargon, no complexity, works across all proficiency levels.

**Audience segmentation is exemplary:**
- Consumers: "Have all your cravings delivered to your doorstep"
- Drivers: "Be your own boss"

Same brand, completely different emotional hooks for different audiences.

**Social proof uses hard numbers and government validation:**
S$5.2 billion contribution to Singapore's economy. Government awards prominently displayed. Economic impact numbers signal institutional-level trust.

**Community framing over individual benefit:**
> "Driving Southeast Asia forward by creating economic empowerment for everyone."

The "for everyone" transforms a corporate mission statement into a collectivist value proposition.

**Key lesson:** Write at Grade 6 and you can serve the entire region. Segment copy by audience, not just by market.

## Wise

**Category:** International money transfers / multi-currency accounts
**Markets:** Global, strong SEA presence (Philippines, Singapore, Malaysia)
**Audience:** Individuals and businesses sending money internationally
**Why their copy works:**

**Most aggressive competitive positioning in SEA fintech:**
> "Banks inflate the mid-market exchange rate to hide fees. Not Wise."

Direct, confrontational toward banks — a strategy that works because banks are universally distrusted in SEA fintech contexts.

**Shows, doesn't tell — real-time savings calculator:**
The hero section embeds a calculator showing exact PHP/SGD/MYR amounts. Users see their actual savings before signing up. Demonstrating value eliminates the need for persuasive copy.

**Grade 6-8 readability — financial concepts without jargon:**
Complex concepts (exchange rates, hidden fees, mid-market rates) explained in plain language. Never assumes financial literacy.

**Speed as differentiator:**
> "The fast way to send money abroad"

One sentence. Clear JTBD. The word "fast" does all the emotional work.

**Key lesson:** If your product saves people money, show the exact amount. Calculators and live data outperform claims. Competitive positioning works when the competitor (banks) is universally seen as the problem.

## StoreHub

**Category:** Retail and F&B POS, business management
**Markets:** Malaysia, Singapore, Philippines, Thailand
**Audience:** SME owners (retail shops, restaurants, service businesses)
**Why their copy works:**

**Master of Before-After-Bridge with parallel structure:**
- "From chaos to control" (F&B)
- "From clutter to clarity" (Retail)
- "From friction to flow" (Services)

Each pair is alliterative, 4-5 words, instantly scannable. The consistency across verticals creates brand memorability.

**Problem-reversal headline:**
> "Run Your Business, Not Your POS"

Textbook JTBD — the "job" is running the business, and the POS should not be the thing that demands attention.

**FAQ sections address real SME anxieties:**
> "Our staff isn't very tech-trained. Will this system be easy to use?"

This acknowledges a genuine SEA concern — many SME staff have limited tech exposure. Addressing it proactively removes a major conversion barrier.

**Key lesson:** Parallel structure (From X to Y) is inherently scannable and works across proficiency levels. FAQ sections that address real anxieties (not generic questions) build trust.

## Xendit

**Category:** Payment infrastructure for developers and enterprises
**Markets:** Indonesia, Philippines, Singapore (expanding SEA)
**Audience:** Developers, CTOs, enterprise finance teams
**Why their copy works:**

**Coined "dynamic markets" as branded terminology:**
Replaced "emerging markets" — a small linguistic choice that reframes the audience positively without condescension. Shows cultural awareness and respect.

**Lists specific local payment rails as proof of depth:**
QRIS, GCash, PromptPay, VietQR, FPX — naming each country's specific payment infrastructure signals genuine local integration, not just a checkbox.

**Anti-chatbot positioning with humor:**
> "Don't get caught in an infinite loop talking to robots"

Differentiates through humor while signaling real human support — a rare example of humor that works in B2B SEA copy because it's functional (addressing a real pain) rather than cultural.

**Speed promise with friction removal:**
> "Try Xendit for free in just 5 minutes!"

Combines the two most powerful SEA conversion levers: free + fast.

**Key lesson:** Naming specific local infrastructure (payment methods, banks, partners) signals depth that generic "we support local payments" claims cannot. Reframing condescending terms shows cultural intelligence.

## Carousell

**Category:** C2C marketplace (secondhand goods)
**Markets:** Singapore, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Philippines, Taiwan
**Audience:** Young consumers (Gen-Z, Millennials)
**Why their copy works:**

**Most informal brand analyzed — and it works because it's authentic:**
> "Every kinda thing, for every kinda person"

Grade 5 readability. "Kinda" is casual without being slangy — accessible across markets.

**Sustainability framing taps Gen-Z values:**
> "Make money while saving the Earth"

Links commerce to environmental consciousness — a values-driven framing that resonates with SEA Gen-Z who are increasingly sustainability-conscious.

**AI features framed through speed, not technology:**
> "Take a photo and our AI fills in the title, description and price"

No jargon about "machine learning" or "computer vision" — just what it does for you, described in one sentence.

**Speed as primary CTA:**
> "LIST IN 30 SECONDS"

Removes complexity barrier with a speed promise. All caps creates urgency without being pushy.

**Singlish deployed strategically in community campaigns:**
> "Don't say I bo jio!" (Don't say I didn't invite you!)

Used in social/community channels only — never on product pages. This is the correct boundary.

**Key lesson:** Casual can work for consumer products if it's genuinely casual (not faking it). Frame AI/tech features through speed and outcomes, never through the technology itself.

## SleekFlow

**Category:** Omnichannel messaging platform
**Markets:** SEA, Hong Kong, global
**Audience:** Sales teams, customer support, marketing teams
**Why their copy works:**

**Effective PAS execution for SEA B2B:**
- Problem: "When your teams work in silos"
- Agitate: "your customer experience suffers. Leads get lost, sales performance is a black box, and support is slow and repetitive"
- Solve: "AI agents engage your visitors 24/7"

The agitation is specific and business-focused — listing actual consequences (lost leads, black box, slow support) rather than emotional manipulation.

**Third-party trust signals prominently displayed:**
Meta Select Tier Business Partner status + G2 badges (4.6, 200+ reviews). Leveraging external validation platforms that SEA B2B buyers increasingly check.

**Category ownership in headline:**
> "Omnichannel messaging platform for AI conversions"

Direct JTBD statement. No fluff. Tells you exactly what it is and what outcome it drives.

**Key lesson:** B2B PAS in SEA works when the Agitate phase lists specific business consequences (quantifiable where possible) rather than emotional pain. Third-party review platform badges are growing in importance for SEA B2B.

## Aspire

**Category:** Business finance platform (accounts, cards, expense management)
**Markets:** Singapore, Indonesia, Thailand, Vietnam (expanding)
**Audience:** Startups, SMEs, growth-stage companies
**Why their copy works:**

**Relatable analogies that work across cultures:**
> "In the past, we used different devices for calls, music, and work. Just like smartphones brought everything together, Aspire elegantly combines corporate cards, FX, business accounts."

Smartphone analogy is universally understood across SEA. No cultural reference that fails in any market.

**Clean, low-friction CTA:**
> "Get Started. It's Free."

Four words. Removes cost barrier and complexity barrier simultaneously.

**Investor naming as credibility signal:**
Sequoia, Lightspeed, Tencent, PayPal, Y Combinator — naming tier-1 investors signals international credibility. Especially effective for startup-audience products.

**Local customer naming for social proof:**
Endowus, Zenyum, Tech In Asia, AirAsia, Love Bonito — recognizable SEA brands that create in-group validation.

**Key lesson:** Universal analogies (smartphones, not cultural references) bridge markets. Naming both investors (international credibility) and customers (local validation) creates two-layer trust.

## Gojek

**Category:** Super-app (ride-hailing, delivery, payments, services)
**Markets:** Indonesia, Singapore, Vietnam
**Audience:** Consumers and service providers
**Why their copy works:**

**Social mission embedded directly in positioning:**
> "An app with a social mission: to improve the welfare and livelihoods of the Indonesian people."

Not CSR added as an afterthought — the mission IS the positioning. In SEA's collectivist cultures, this social framing builds trust that feature-focused copy cannot.

**Proper language segmentation:**
English copy is restrained and warm. Indonesian version uses colloquialisms like "buuaannyak." Different registers for different languages — not translations of each other.

**Holiday-themed content shows cultural integration:**
Ramadan, Eid content demonstrates deep understanding of cultural rhythms, not surface-level marketing.

**Key lesson:** If your product has genuine social impact, lead with it — in SEA, social mission is a trust multiplier, not a distraction from the product pitch.

## Shopee

**Category:** E-commerce marketplace
**Markets:** Pan-SEA, Taiwan, Brazil
**Audience:** Mass-market consumers
**Why their copy works:**

**Simplicity converts at scale:**
> "Cheaper, Faster On Shopee"

Two value propositions in four words. No need for elaborate messaging when the benefits are this clear.

**Numbered sale events as their own shorthand:**
9.9, 10.10, 11.11, 12.12 — these have become a cultural calendar across SEA. The numbers alone trigger purchase intent without any copy at all. Demonstrates how repetition builds its own language.

**UGC as copy strategy:**
#ShopeeFinds and #BudolFinds: 16.4 billion TikTok views through user-generated content. The best copy isn't written by the brand — it's facilitated by the brand and written by users.

**Key lesson:** When your value proposition is genuinely simple, say it simply. Creating cultural events (numbered sales) that users internalize is more powerful than any individual campaign.

## Funding Societies

**Category:** SME lending platform
**Markets:** Singapore, Indonesia (as Modalku), Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam
**Audience:** SME owners needing working capital
**Why their copy works:**

**Effective dual-market branding:**
"Funding Societies" for English-speaking markets, "Modalku" ("My Capital") for Indonesia — a complete rebrand, not a translation. Shows commitment to each market rather than forcing one brand name across different languages.

**Speed-first CTA addressing the primary pain:**
> "Lightning-fast loans up to S$150K, disbursed in 24 hours"

SME owners' primary pain is speed of access to capital, not interest rates. This CTA leads with what matters most.

**Key lesson:** Consider separate brand names for markets with different languages rather than forcing a single English brand name everywhere. Lead CTAs with the primary pain point for your specific audience.

## Jitta

**Category:** Investment analytics platform
**Markets:** Thailand, expanding SEA
**Audience:** Retail investors, value investing enthusiasts
**Why their copy works:**

**Bridges Western concepts with local education:**
References Warren Buffett and value investing (universally recognized) while creating educational content that fills local financial literacy gaps.

**Proprietary scoring creates branded copy hooks:**
"Jitta Score" and "Jitta Line" — branded intellectual property that doubles as copy shorthand. Once users understand the score, all future copy can reference it without explanation.

**Key lesson:** Creating proprietary metrics or scores gives you branded terminology that becomes a communication shorthand — reduces copy length while increasing comprehension for returning users.

## Lazada

**Category:** E-commerce marketplace
**Markets:** Pan-SEA (Alibaba-backed)
**Why their copy is a cautionary tale:**

**Dual positioning (success):**
- Aspirational: "Go Where Your Heart Beats"
- Utilitarian: "Always The Better Price & Free Shipping"

Covering both emotional and rational buyers is smart — different users respond to different appeals.

**Thailand controversy (failure):**
A TikTok video perceived as mocking Princess Chulabhorn triggered a Royal Thai Army-led consumer boycott. This is SEA's most cited example of cultural misread risk.

The lesson isn't "don't do TikTok in Thailand." It's that cultural sensitivities in SEA are specific, local, and can be severe. Royalty in Thailand, religious references in Indonesia/Malaysia, political sensitivities in Vietnam — each market has its own absolute no-go zones that require local team members to navigate.

**Key lesson:** Cultural review by local team members is not optional polish — it is a prerequisite for not losing money. A campaign that works in Singapore can cause a boycott in Thailand.

## Common Anti-Patterns

### Over-Americanized copy
Direct, aggressive, promotional language that works in Western markets feels jarring in SEA. "Just Do It" required softening for Asian markets. SaaS companies using US-centric idioms ("hit the ground running," "move the needle," "drink the Kool-Aid") create comprehension barriers and cultural disconnect simultaneously.

### Translation artifacts
HashMicro's "beyond your expectation" (should be "expectations") — grammatically imperfect copy undermines premium positioning. If your product costs $500/month, your copy cannot contain ESL errors. This is especially damaging because it signals the company doesn't invest in its SEA presence.

### Assuming uniform language works across markets
Malay and Bahasa Indonesia share linguistic roots but differ significantly in vocabulary and grammar. Similarly, "standard English" varies by market — copy that feels natural in Singapore may feel too formal for the Philippines or too casual for Vietnam's professional segment.

### Color and symbol blindness
Pepsi lost SEA market share by changing to light blue — associated with death and mourning in parts of the region. Pepsodent promoted teeth whitening in regions where darkened teeth are a social status signal. IKEA product names that sounded crude in Thai. Always screen visual and linguistic choices through local lenses.

### Credit-card-only checkout
Over 60% of SEA's population remains unbanked. One SaaS founder reported losing 60% of potential customers with Stripe-only checkout; adding local payment methods increased revenue 3.5x. The payment stack must include digital wallets, bank transfers, QR codes, and increasingly BNPL.

### Linear funnel assumption
Traditional marketing funnels assume rational, sequential journeys. In SEA, consumer behavior is non-linear — people forward links on WhatsApp, seek validation in Facebook Groups, consult local creators, and circle back multiple times before purchasing. SEA purchase journeys are "more like spaghetti" than funnels. Design copy for shareability and re-entry, not for pushing users down a linear path.

### Paragraph-as-headline
A real example: "Combined HR, Payroll, Expense and Business Travel Management applications as a solution in managing HR & Finance administration that is accurate, real-time and easy to use anytime, anywhere." — 32 words, zero scan value, reads like a product requirements document.

### "Leading platform" inflation
When every competitor claims to be "the leading platform," the phrase becomes meaningless. If you must claim leadership, back it with specific proof. Otherwise, let your social proof numbers and customer logos speak for themselves.
